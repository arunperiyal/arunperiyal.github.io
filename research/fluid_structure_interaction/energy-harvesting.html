Project Overview

This research focuses on developing and analyzing energy harvesting devices that utilize vortex-induced vibrations (VIV) to convert fluid flow energy into electrical power. The study investigates the parametric dependencies and system stability characteristics that are crucial for optimizing energy extraction efficiency in marine and offshore applications.

VIV Phenomena

Understanding vortex shedding patterns and their influence on oscillatory behavior

Power Generation

Optimizing energy conversion through electromagnetic and piezoelectric systems

Stability Analysis

Investigating dynamic stability and bifurcation behavior of the harvester system

Research Objectives

  • Conduct parametric studies on system mass ratio, damping, and stiffness effects
  • Analyze lock-in regions and resonance conditions for maximum power extraction
  • Investigate nonlinear dynamics and stability boundaries of the harvester
  • Develop predictive models for performance optimization across varying flow conditions
  • Evaluate practical implementation challenges in offshore and marine environments

Methodology

The research employs a combination of computational fluid dynamics (CFD) simulations, finite element analysis (FEA), and experimental validation. Wake oscillator models and reduced-order modeling techniques are used to capture the essential physics while maintaining computational efficiency. Stability analysis is performed using Lyapunov exponents and bifurcation diagrams to identify optimal operating regimes.

Key Findings

The parametric study reveals critical relationships between system parameters and energy harvesting efficiency. Lower mass ratios and optimized damping coefficients significantly enhance power output within the lock-in region. The stability analysis identifies parameter ranges where the system exhibits steady oscillations suitable for sustained energy extraction. Bifurcation analysis demonstrates the existence of multiple solution branches and potential hysteresis effects that must be considered in practical implementations.

Applications

  • Powering remote ocean sensors and monitoring equipment
  • Autonomous underwater vehicles (AUV) battery charging systems
  • Marine renewable energy installations
  • Self-powered offshore structural health monitoring systems

Future Work

Future research will focus on developing adaptive control strategies for varying flow conditions, investigating multi-degree-of-freedom harvesters for enhanced energy capture, and conducting long-term field testing in real ocean environments. Additionally, machine learning approaches will be explored for real-time optimization and predictive maintenance of energy harvesting systems.
