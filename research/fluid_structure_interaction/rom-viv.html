Project Overview

This research develops efficient reduced-order models (ROMs) for predicting vortex-induced vibrations (VIV) in marine structures using advanced machine learning techniques, particularly Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs). Traditional high-fidelity finite element and computational fluid dynamics simulations are computationally expensive, limiting their use in real-time monitoring, optimization, and design exploration. This work aims to create accurate, fast-running surrogate models that capture essential VIV physics while dramatically reducing computational requirements.

Research Objectives

  • Develop Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) for VIV prediction incorporating governing equations
  • Reduce high degree-of-freedom FEM problems to efficient low-dimensional representations
  • Create data-efficient training approaches combining simulation and experimental data
  • Validate ROM accuracy against high-fidelity simulations and experimental results
  • Enable real-time VIV prediction for design optimization and structural health monitoring

Methodology

The research employs Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) that incorporate the Navier-Stokes equations, structural dynamics equations, and boundary conditions directly into the loss function. This approach ensures that predictions respect fundamental physical laws even in regions with sparse training data. Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD) and Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) techniques are used to identify dominant modes and create efficient reduced-order representations. Transfer learning strategies enable model adaptation across different Reynolds numbers, mass ratios, and damping conditions with minimal retraining.

Key Innovations

The developed PINNs achieve 3-4 orders of magnitude speedup compared to full CFD-FEM simulations while maintaining prediction errors below 5%. The physics-informed approach requires significantly less training data than purely data-driven methods, making it practical for problems where high-fidelity simulation data is expensive to generate. The models successfully capture lock-in phenomena, higher harmonic responses, and transition between response branches that are critical for accurate VIV prediction. Uncertainty quantification capabilities provide confidence bounds on predictions, essential for risk-informed decision making.

ROM Architecture

  • Multi-layer perceptron networks with residual connections for deep architectures
  • Physics-informed loss functions incorporating PDE residuals and boundary conditions
  • Automatic differentiation for efficient gradient computation of physics terms
  • Encoder-decoder architectures for learning efficient latent representations
  • Ensemble methods for uncertainty quantification and robust predictions

Applications

The reduced-order models enable rapid design space exploration for VIV suppression devices, allowing engineers to evaluate thousands of configurations in hours rather than months. Real-time prediction capabilities support digital twin implementations for offshore structures, enabling continuous monitoring and early warning systems. The models facilitate integration of VIV considerations into multi-objective optimization frameworks for riser and pipeline design. Educational applications include interactive simulation tools for teaching fluid-structure interaction concepts without requiring expensive computational resources.

Validation and Testing

Comprehensive validation against benchmark experimental data from towing tank and wind tunnel tests demonstrates excellent agreement across various flow conditions and structural configurations. Blind predictions on unseen test cases verify the models' generalization capabilities. Comparison with commercial VIV prediction software shows comparable or superior accuracy at a fraction of the computational cost. Sensitivity analysis identifies key parameters influencing predictions and guides data collection for model refinement.

Future Developments

Ongoing work extends the ROM framework to multi-span risers with spatial variation in currents and structural properties. Integration with optimization algorithms will enable automated design of VIV suppression strategies. Development of online learning capabilities will allow models to continuously improve as new monitoring data becomes available. Extension to other fluid-structure interaction problems including galloping, buffeting, and flutter will demonstrate the generality of the PINN-based ROM approach.
