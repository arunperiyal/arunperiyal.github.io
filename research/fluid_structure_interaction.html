Research Overview

Fluid-structure interaction (FSI) represents one of the most challenging and important areas in marine engineering. My research focuses on understanding and predicting the coupled behavior between flowing fluids and flexible structures, particularly in offshore and marine applications. This involves developing computational frameworks, conducting experimental validation, and creating reduced-order models that capture essential physics while remaining computationally tractable for engineering design.

Research Focus Areas

  • Vortex-Induced Vibrations: Investigating lock-in, amplitude response, frequency behavior, and fatigue damage in flexible cylinders subjected to cross-flow
  • Computational FSI: Developing efficient coupling algorithms using CFD and FEM with advanced mesh motion techniques and interface treatment
  • VIV Suppression: Designing and evaluating passive and active suppression devices including helical strakes, fairings, and grooved surfaces
  • Multi-span Risers: Analyzing complex FSI in multi-span configurations with varying currents and soil interaction
  • Turbulence Modeling: Evaluating RANS, LES, and hybrid approaches for accurate prediction of unsteady fluid forces

Methodological Approaches

My research employs a multi-pronged approach combining high-fidelity simulations, reduced-order modeling, and experimental validation. For high-fidelity analysis, I use coupled CFD-FEM frameworks with two-way interaction to capture the full physics. The structural domain is modeled using nonlinear beam elements or shell elements depending on the problem, while the fluid domain employs Large Eddy Simulation or hybrid RANS-LES turbulence models. To make the analysis tractable for design purposes, I develop reduced-order models based on wake oscillators, proper orthogonal decomposition, and physics-informed neural networks.

Key Findings and Contributions

Through systematic parametric studies, I have identified critical mass-damping combinations that govern VIV response regimes. My work on helical groove suppression devices has demonstrated up to 70% reduction in vibration amplitudes through disruption of spanwise vortex correlation. I have developed efficient partitioned FSI coupling schemes that maintain stability while reducing computational cost by an order of magnitude compared to monolithic approaches. Additionally, my research on wake oscillator calibration has produced improved models that accurately capture higher harmonic responses and transition between response branches.

Applications and Impact

The FSI research has direct applications in offshore oil and gas industry for riser design, pipeline installation, and fatigue life prediction. The VIV suppression technologies developed through this research are being considered for implementation on deepwater drilling risers, potentially saving millions in maintenance costs and preventing catastrophic failures. The reduced-order models enable rapid design space exploration and real-time monitoring applications. Furthermore, the computational frameworks developed are applicable to other FSI problems including wind turbine blade dynamics and aircraft flutter.

Ongoing and Future Work

Current research focuses on developing machine learning-enhanced FSI models that can predict responses with minimal computational cost while maintaining high accuracy. I am also investigating multi-physics coupling that includes thermal effects and erosion, which are important for subsea pipelines. Future work will extend the FSI framework to include uncertainty quantification, enabling probabilistic design approaches. Collaboration with industry partners will facilitate full-scale validation and technology transfer of VIV suppression devices.
