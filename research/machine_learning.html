Research Overview

My machine learning research bridges artificial intelligence and marine engineering, developing data-driven approaches that complement traditional physics-based methods. The focus is on creating interpretable, physics-informed models that leverage the strengths of both machine learning and domain expertise. Applications span predictive modeling, optimization, anomaly detection, and real-time decision support for complex marine systems where traditional analytical solutions are intractable.

Research Areas

  • Physics-Informed Neural Networks: Developing PINNs that incorporate governing PDEs as soft constraints, enabling accurate predictions with limited training data
  • Reduced-Order Modeling: Using autoencoders, POD, and DMD with machine learning to create efficient low-dimensional representations of high-fidelity models
  • Predictive Maintenance: Applying anomaly detection and time series forecasting for early fault detection in marine machinery and structures
  • Design Optimization: Integrating Bayesian optimization, genetic algorithms, and neural networks for multi-objective ship and offshore structure design
  • Uncertainty Quantification: Employing Gaussian processes and ensemble methods to quantify prediction uncertainties in engineering applications

Methodologies and Techniques

The research employs diverse ML architectures tailored to specific problem characteristics. For spatiotemporal problems like VIV, I use convolutional-LSTM networks that capture both spatial patterns and temporal dynamics. Physics-informed neural networks embed conservation laws and boundary conditions directly in the loss function, ensuring physically consistent predictions. For optimization, I apply Bayesian approaches that efficiently explore design spaces using Gaussian process surrogates. Transfer learning enables adapting pre-trained models to new scenarios with limited data, critical for expensive marine experiments.

Key Innovations and Results

My PINN-based approach for VIV prediction achieves 95% accuracy with 90% less training data compared to purely data-driven methods by leveraging fluid dynamics equations. I have developed hybrid models combining wake oscillators with neural network corrections that improve prediction accuracy by 40% across diverse flow conditions. For ship resistance prediction, ensemble methods integrating multiple empirical formulations with gradient boosting achieve errors below 3% on unseen designs. Additionally, real-time anomaly detection systems I developed can identify bearing faults 2-3 weeks before failure using vibration signatures.

Data Sources and Datasets

  • High-fidelity CFD simulations (50,000+ runs for various geometries)
  • Experimental data from towing tank and wave basin tests
  • Field measurements from instrumented offshore structures
  • Publicly available ship performance databases (ITTC, KRISO)
  • Synthetic data generation using physics-based simulators

Software Frameworks and Tools

My research utilizes PyTorch and TensorFlow for deep learning model development, with custom layers for physics-informed constraints. Scikit-learn provides baseline ML algorithms and preprocessing pipelines. For Bayesian optimization, I employ GPyOpt and Ax platforms. Data processing leverages Pandas and NumPy, while visualization uses Matplotlib, Seaborn, and interactive Plotly dashboards. All code follows reproducible research principles with version control, containerization, and comprehensive documentation.

Industry Applications and Impact

The ML models have been deployed in several industrial applications. A predictive maintenance system for thruster bearings is operational on 15 offshore support vessels, reducing unplanned downtime by 35%. Ship resistance prediction tools are integrated into a major shipyard's design workflow, accelerating the preliminary design phase by 70%. Digital twin implementations using ML-based state estimation provide real-time monitoring of riser integrity for subsea operations. These applications demonstrate substantial cost savings while improving safety and operational efficiency.

Challenges and Ongoing Work

Key challenges include ensuring model robustness to out-of-distribution scenarios, addressing data scarcity in novel marine applications, and improving interpretability for regulatory acceptance. Current work focuses on developing explainable AI techniques that provide insight into model decisions, critical for safety-critical applications. I am also investigating federated learning approaches that enable collaborative model training across multiple organizations while preserving data privacy. Integration of reinforcement learning for adaptive control of marine systems represents another promising direction.
