Research Overview

My research in nonlinear dynamics focuses on understanding complex behavior in engineering systems, particularly in marine and offshore structures. Nonlinear phenomena such as bifurcations, chaos, and multi-stability play crucial roles in determining system response under realistic operating conditions. By employing analytical, numerical, and experimental methods, I investigate how system parameters influence dynamic behavior and develop strategies for predicting and controlling nonlinear responses.

Research Areas

  • Parametric Excitation: Investigating parametrically excited marine structures including Mathieu and Hill equations, stability boundaries, and transition to chaos
  • Multi-stable Systems: Analyzing systems with multiple coexisting attractors and basin erosion phenomena relevant to offshore structures
  • Synchronization: Studying synchronization and lock-in phenomena in coupled oscillators and fluid-structure interaction
  • Perturbation Methods: Applying multiple scales, averaging, and harmonic balance techniques to weakly nonlinear systems
  • Stochastic Dynamics: Examining effects of random excitation on nonlinear system behavior and escape from potential wells

Key Contributions

My work has revealed important insights into the nonlinear dynamics of marine risers under combined parametric and direct excitation. Through bifurcation analysis, I have identified parameter regions where sudden transitions in response amplitude occur, which has implications for fatigue life prediction. I have developed reduced-order models that capture essential nonlinear features while enabling rapid analysis for design purposes. Additionally, my research on chaotic dynamics has demonstrated scenarios where traditional linear analysis fails to predict actual system behavior, highlighting the importance of nonlinear approaches in marine engineering.

Applications

The nonlinear dynamics research has direct applications in predicting and preventing catastrophic failures in offshore structures. Understanding bifurcation behavior helps identify safe operating envelopes where large amplitude responses are avoided. The analysis of multi-stability informs risk assessment by quantifying the probability of jumps between response states. Insights from chaos theory guide the design of control strategies that exploit or suppress chaotic behavior as needed. These findings contribute to improved safety standards and operational guidelines for marine and offshore industries.

Future Directions

Future research will explore machine learning approaches for detecting early warning signs of bifurcations from time series data. I am also investigating network dynamics to understand how nonlinear interactions propagate through complex offshore structural systems. Experimental validation of theoretical predictions in controlled laboratory settings remains a priority. Additionally, extending the analysis to high-dimensional systems using manifold-based techniques will enable more realistic modeling of industrial-scale structures.
